1920s Jazz Era

“It don’t mean a thing if it ain’t got that swing.” — Duke Ellington

An Era of Rhythm, Rebellion, and Cultural Revolution

“The Jazz Age was a vibrant cultural movement that redefined music, identity, and freedom through improvisation, innovation, and social transformation.”

KEY CONCEPT: IMPROVISATION & EXPRESSION

Jazz celebrated spontaneity. Unlike classical music, it encouraged performers to invent and reinterpret on the spot, turning each performance into a unique artistic moment. This focus on individual voice and emotional depth helped shape modern music and artistic freedom.

3 ICONIC FIGURES OF JAZZ

Louis Armstrong

Known for: Trumpet genius and vocal charisma
Armstrong’s groundbreaking solos and gravelly voice revolutionized jazz performance, bringing the genre to a global audience.

Duke Ellington

Known for: Big band leadership and sophisticated compositions
A composer and bandleader, Ellington elevated jazz to new artistic heights, blending classical elements with bold rhythms.

Billie Holiday

Known for: Emotional depth and lyrical interpretation
Holiday’s voice conveyed raw emotion and social struggle, especially in songs like “Strange Fruit,” making her a powerful voice for civil rights.
