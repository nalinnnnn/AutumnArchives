MAUSOLEUM AT HALICARNASSUS

~A tomb fit not just for a ruler, but for immortality itself.~

“Such was the magnificence of Mausolus’s tomb that all who saw it called it one of the greatest wonders ever built.” – Ancient Greek traveler

The Mausoleum at Halicarnassus was a monumental tomb built around 350 B.C. for Mausolus, a Persian satrap (governor) of Caria, and his wife (and sister) Artemisia II. Towering at nearly 45 meters, it was an architectural fusion of Greek, Egyptian, and Lycian styles — so influential that the word "mausoleum" still refers to grand tombs today.

The Mausoleum stood on a rectangular platform, with 36 Ionic columns supporting a stepped pyramid. On top was a chariot statue of Mausolus and Artemisia. The tomb was adorned with life-sized sculptural reliefs by four famous Greek sculptors: Scopas, Leochares, Bryaxis, and Timotheus — each responsible for a side of the building.
